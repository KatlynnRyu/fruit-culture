2.1

Performance and Confidence Transfer

Performing arts are widely believed to build confidence that extends beyond the stage. The intuition has a basis in Bandura's concept of self-efficacy: succeeding at a scary task under safe conditions strengthens belief in your own abilities — and that belief can travel to other settings.

Yet the research record is split. A controlled experiment with 237 elementary students (Freeman, Sullivan & Fulton, 2003) found no significant effect of creative drama on self-concept — though the authors admitted their broad scales may have missed real changes the drama instructor saw in shy students. Meanwhile, a study of over 2,000 students (Burton, Horowitz & Abeles, 2000) found arts-rich students consistently more confident and more willing to take risks — gains that surfaced most clearly in the qualitative data, not the standardized measures.

So the disagreement may be less about whether performing builds confidence than about how confidence is measured. When studies listen to young people directly, the effect becomes visible — and our interviews found the same pattern:

2.4

Performing as Becoming Someone Else: The Mask That Frees

Several participants described a paradox: performing as someone else made it easier to be themselves. Goffman (1959) argued that everyday life is already a performance — we present a version of ourselves to an audience and worry whether it will be accepted. If ordinary life is a role, then stepping into an explicit, scripted role on stage may relieve that pressure, because the performer is no longer presenting their own self for judgment.

Drama therapists observe exactly this: under the cover of "just acting," the character becomes a shield — protection rather than disguise. Within that safe distance, adolescents can take emotional risks without feeling that they are being evaluated.

On stage you're “someone else, not necessarily yourself” — the character creates distance from the part of you that fears being watched.Student E
Theater as a way to express emotions she normally could not access — the character as a vehicle for feelings she would otherwise hold back.Student J

For both students, the role was not a mask that hid them but one that freed them. The character becomes a kind of permission slip — a way to feel and show what the unprotected self cannot. And for a young person taught to suppress emotion, a character may offer the rare cover under which those emotions can finally be expressed.

2.5

Cultural Scripts and Adolescent Wellbeing

Emotional expression is not just personality — it is taught. Hochschild (1979) named the unwritten "feeling rules" that govern which emotions we're expected to show, and Röttger-Rössler et al. (2015) showed that communities actively train children in which emotions to express and which to suppress. Our three cultures teach three distinct scripts:

Korea
restraint

Children learn to adjust or suppress emotion by social context — nunchi, chemyon, humility, deflecting compliments.

United States
open expression

Individualistic cultures endorse greater emotional expressivity — a 32-country survey of 5,000+ people (Matsumoto et al., 2008).

Mexico
warm positivity

Collectivistic, but organized around simpatía: warm, open expression of positive emotion and harmony (Salvador et al., 2020).

These differences track measurable gaps in how adolescents fare. In PISA 2022, Korean fifteen-year-olds reported life satisfaction of 6.36 — ranking 66th of 73 countries, while Mexican adolescents in earlier rounds scored above the OECD average. Cultural scripts are only one factor among many, but a culture that teaches young people to suppress what they feel may offer fewer outlets than one that encourages open expression.

“If you want to cry, just cry.”participant raised in a Latin American context

This thread ran clearly through our interviews: participants from Korean and Chinese backgrounds described being taught to stay humble and avoid attention, while performing arts often became the rare space where that restraint could be set aside.

How We Did It (Methods)

Design

Qualitative — semi-structured interviews with thematic analysis, chosen because standardized scales have missed changes performers describe in their own words.

Participants

15 adolescents & young adults: 12 performers (theater, dance, music, voice) + 3 non-performers. Korean, Chinese, American, Indian American, and Latin American backgrounds.

Interviews

Four parts: performance history · confidence & its transfer · body and self-perception · cultural and family messages about emotion.

Analysis

Thematic coding matrix (confidence transfer, body as tool vs. judged object, cultural restraint, role distance) plus a quote bank of representative statements.

Ethics

Informed consent for all participants; minors protected with pseudonyms (Student A–O). Real names appear nowhere in the analysis.

Limitations

Small convenience sample and a tiny comparison group — patterns are suggestive, not statistical. All data self-reported.

About Us 🎭

This website presents a qualitative research study on performing arts and adolescent development. Fifteen adolescents and young adults were interviewed — twelve performers in theater, dance, instrumental music, and vocal performance, plus three non-performers as a comparison group.

What we asked

Semi-structured interviews covered four areas: participants' history with performance; whether confidence developed on stage carried into everyday life; how performing shaped body image and self-perception; and what messages participants' cultures and families gave them about expressing emotion.

What we found

Six of nine performers described confidence that transferred into presentations and social life — none of the three non-performers did. Many described a paradox: performing as someone else made it easier to be themselves. And a cultural thread ran through it all: participants raised with emotional restraint often found in performance a rare space where those feelings could finally be expressed.

A note on the sample

This was a small convenience sample, so the patterns here are suggestive, not statistical. All participants are identified by pseudonyms (Student A–O) to protect their privacy.
